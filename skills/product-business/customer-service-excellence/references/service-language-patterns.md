# Service Language Patterns

Service language is built from a small set of patterns, each engineered to do a specific job. Words matter, but the form of the construction matters more: a customer who hears the right form in the wrong tone of voice is not soothed, and a customer who hears warm tone but a defensive form does not feel heard. Each pattern below has a structure, a phrase form, and a list of failure modes.

## Pattern 1 - Empathy statement (the foundation)

Form: name the emotion + name the source of the emotion. Be specific.

Examples that work because they name both halves:
- "It seems like you are pretty upset by the delay."
- "I know it can be frustrating to have to complete these forms."
- "You must have been pretty upset to find out the product didn't work."

Failure modes:
- General statements: "I see where you're coming from." / "I totally hear you." (no emotion named, no source named).
- Pure emotion-naming with no source: "You sound frustrated." (the customer hears it as a label).
- Pure source-naming with no emotion: "The delay was unfortunate." (sounds like the agent is moving on without acknowledging the feeling).

The empathy statement is also the primary defusing tool when a customer is escalating, and the primary opener after a long wait, a transferred call, or a known process failure (preemptive strike).

## Pattern 2 - Acknowledge without encouraging

Form: empathy statement + immediate refocus to the problem, in the same breath.

Example: "I can see you are angry about the product problem. Let's get back to what we can do to help you - I can suggest a few things that might help."

The principle: what you focus on, you get more of. Pure acknowledgment without refocus invites more emotion. Pure refocus without acknowledgment is heard as dismissal. The pair-and-pivot pattern is the safest default.

Failure modes:
- Pausing for several seconds between the empathy statement and the refocus (the customer fills the gap with more emotion).
- Using a transition word that sounds dismissive ("but", "however", "anyway"). Use "let's" or "and" - they connect rather than contrast.

## Pattern 3 - Apology

Form: short, sincere, specific, paired with refocus.

Examples:
- "Mr. Jones, I'm really sorry. It looks like I'm going to be about 10 minutes late because there's been a delay in printing the contracts. I'm printing them out now and shouldn't be later than 3 p.m."
- "I apologise if you've been having problems with our phone system - I am going to help you."

Apologising on behalf of the company does not concede culpability; admitting the possibility of an error does not concede culpability either. The point is to defuse, not to litigate.

Failure modes:
- "I'm sorry, but..." - the "but" reads as defensive and erases the apology.
- Long, repeated apologies: amplify the emotion they are trying to discharge.
- Insincere or perfunctory tone: worse than no apology - actively angers customers because the customer hears it as scripted.

## Pattern 4 - Assurance of effort vs. assurance of results

Form (effort): "I'm going to do my best to [specific outcome with a soft qualifier]."
Form (results): "I will [specific outcome] by [specific time]."

Use effort when the outcome is uncertain ("I can see you're in a hurry, and I'm going to do my best to get this wrapped up in a few minutes"). Use results only when you can legitimately guarantee.

Failure modes:
- Promising results then under-delivering: the recovery is harder than the original issue.
- Vague effort statements ("we'll see what we can do"): customers hear them as deflection.

## Pattern 5 - Refocus statement

Form: acknowledge the side-issue briefly, then redirect to the actionable problem.

Example: "I can see you're angry about how the call was handled. Let's get back to what we can do to help you. I can suggest a few things that might help."

Use after the customer has begun straying into criticism of the company, the agent personally, or other tangential complaints. The refocus is what makes the empathy statement productive.

## Pattern 6 - Active listening / Summarising

Form: rephrase the customer's key points and reflect them back, often as a question.

Examples:
- "So, you're saying that you're sure there are parts missing from the product and you want a complete refund. Is that right?"
- "Let me make sure I understand - the order arrived on the 12th, the second item was missing, and you've already called twice. Is that the situation?"

Use at the end of the customer's first long statement and again before proposing a resolution. Summarising verbally can be paired with a written takeaway.

The function of summarising is twofold: it proves you listened, and it catches the cases where both parties believed they understood the same thing and did not.

## Pattern 7 - Probing question (single-issue, sequential)

Form: each question covers one issue and is directly tied to the answer to the previous question.

Example:
- "I see you are browsing the [product range]. Are you interested in more information about them?"
- (Yes.) "Did you have an interest in a particular [size / variant]?"
- (Specific answer.) "What features matter most to you?"

The pattern signals listening, because each question is built on the customer's last answer. Multi-issue questions ("what's wrong, when did it start, and what have you tried?") overwhelm and produce defensive single-line answers.

## Pattern 8 - Verbal softeners

Form: replace absolute language with softened language.

Examples:
- "We never make those kinds of mistakes." → "It's unlikely we've made a mistake."
- "Clearly you've made a mistake." → "Perhaps you've misinterpreted something here."

Softeners include: perhaps, sometimes, it's possible, occasionally, unlikely, may have. Use them when correcting a customer's understanding without contradicting them outright.

## Pattern 9 - Face-saving out

Form: take responsibility for the misunderstanding even when it is not yours, to avoid pointing out the customer's deficiency.

Example: "Perhaps the written material isn't very clear, or I haven't explained myself well, so let me see if I can talk you through this, since it can get complicated."

Use when the customer has misunderstood something obvious or made a small error and is at risk of embarrassment. Embarrassing or humiliating a customer is always damaging, even when the customer is in the wrong.

## Pattern 10 - Setting limits

Form: "if [behaviour], then [consequence]" + choice statement.

Example:
- "If you continue to use that language, I'm going to have to end this conversation. It's up to you whether you'd like to continue."
- (If continued.) "I'm going to end this conversation now. You are welcome to call back at some other time."

Required components:
1. Specific identification of the behaviour to stop.
2. The consequence stated calmly.
3. A choice statement that frames the consequence as the customer's decision, not a punishment.

Use only as a last resort, when other techniques have failed. Handle the entire process calmly so it does not feel personal. Be sure of organisational policy on what constitutes grounds for ending an interaction before using.

## Pattern 11 - Offering choices / Empowering

Form: present two or more options the customer can pick between.

Examples:
- "You can contact me by phone or email, whatever is more convenient."
- "Would you prefer I call you back this afternoon, or first thing tomorrow morning?"

Customers who feel choiceless become aggressive; customers who feel they are choosing actively cooperate. Even small choices (coffee or tea) build cooperation.

## Pattern 12 - Voice tone, emphatic

The same words read flat or emphatic carry different meaning. "I understand, I will get this done for you" is service. "I understand, I will get this done for you" with stress on "will" is commitment. Match the customer's energy: a customer in a hurry needs a faster, more emphatic voice; a customer in distress needs a slower, calmer one.

## Patterns to avoid

- "Calm down." / "There's no need to get upset." - these escalate.
- "Policy says..." / "It's company policy." - explain the reasoning, not the policy name.
- "I can't help you with that." (without naming who can) - feels like buck-passing.
- "Per our records..." - reads as bureaucratic gatekeeping.
- "Unfortunately..." - low-information softener that signals a no without explanation.
- Hot or emotional words echoed back ("if you really cared about me" → "when did you start thinking I didn't care about you?" repeats the loaded word and re-arms the customer; rephrase in neutral language).

## Decision rules

- Always pair empathy with refocus.
- Always state apologies short and pair with action.
- Always make probing questions single-issue and sequential.
- When in doubt about absolute language, soften it.
- When the customer is in danger of embarrassment, take responsibility yourself.
- Set limits only after other tools have failed; do it calmly, with a choice frame.

## Source Grounding

- The Empathy Statements requirement to name both the emotion and the specific source ("the delay", "the forms", "the product failure") rather than using general statements.
- The Acknowledging-Without-Encouraging pairing of empathy with refocus, and the principle that "what you focus on, you get more of".
- The Apologize tool with the constraint that apologies must be sincere, short, and paired with refocus, and that perfunctory apologies are worse than none.
- The Assurance-of-Effort versus Assurance-of-Results distinction.
- The Verbal-Softeners list (perhaps, unlikely, occasionally) for replacing absolute language.
- The Setting-Limits "if-then" + choice-statement structure.
- The Voice-Tone-Emphatic principle that emphatic delivery on commitment words ("will") signals commitment more strongly than the words alone.
