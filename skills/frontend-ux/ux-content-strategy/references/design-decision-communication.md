# Design Decision Communication

Most design decisions die not in the design phase but in the meeting where they are presented. This file is the engine's working playbook for getting design decisions agreed to: how to prepare, how to design the meeting itself, how to listen well enough to respond well, and how to manage the part of the meeting that happens *after* the meeting.

Derived from Greever, *Articulating Design Decisions*.

## The Premise: The Whole Book Is About One Meeting

Greever frames the entire problem around a specific event: a meeting in which a designer presents work to non-designer stakeholders or clients. Everything — preparation, listening, response, follow-up — is organized before, during, and after that meeting.

Two assumptions are load-bearing:

1. **The design is correct.** The book assumes the designer has done the research, made good decisions, and the design is the right thing. No amount of articulation rescues a bad design.
2. **Stakeholders are people.** The book's chapter on stakeholders is explicit — they have values, stories, and constraints of their own. Persuasion fails when stakeholders are treated as obstacles.

If either assumption is wrong, this playbook does not apply.

## Stakeholders Are People Too

Before walking into the meeting, the designer should be able to write down, for each stakeholder:

- **Stakeholder values** — what this person measures themselves on (revenue, on-time launch, brand consistency, regulatory exposure). Decisions land when they are framed in those terms.
- **Stakeholder stories** — the narrative the stakeholder tells about how they got to this role and what they have personally invested in adjacent decisions. A logo redesign is never just a logo redesign to the person who chose the original.

If the designer cannot answer these for the room, they are unprepared regardless of how good the deck is.

## Anticipate Reactions

Greever's preparation rule: write down anticipated reactions before the meeting. The simplified frame is *common sense + observed reactions = predictable behavior*. It is not literally that simple, but it gets more predictable with practice.

For each significant design decision, list:

- The reaction you expect from each stakeholder.
- Why you expect it (their values, their prior reactions, their constraints).
- The response you will give.

Bring **alternative designs**, **data**, and **research** when the response would otherwise be opinion against opinion. Without alternatives, "this design vs. nothing" gives stakeholders no productive way to engage; with alternatives, they can be enrolled in the choice.

## Design The Meeting

The book treats the meeting itself as a designed artifact. Make it usable.

### Set Context At The Start

Open the meeting with: the goal of the project, where you are in the process, and what kinds of feedback are useful at this stage. Make explicit that the purpose is to get support to move forward, not to riff on whatever is on screen.

### Name The Meeting Honestly

A meeting titled "Feedback session" invites unbounded reaction. A meeting titled "[Team] — [Project] — Decision review" tells stakeholders what they are walking into and what is expected. Avoid implying the purpose is solely for them to give feedback.

### Use Primacy And Recency

People remember the first and last things you say and forget the middle. Break the content into smaller chunks with distinct transitions. Put the most important framing at the open and the close.

### Remove Distractions

The pharmaceutical-app anecdote in the book — a stakeholder who tried for part of the meeting to read the *lorem ipsum* aloud — is the canonical reminder. Anything in the meeting that pulls attention away from the decision is a tax on the decision. This includes outdated mockups, irrelevant slides, and placeholder copy that looks meaningful.

## Listen — Implicit And Explicit

After preparation, the next-most-important skill is listening. Greever splits it into two:

### Implicit Listening

Skills that happen inside your head and posture without explicitly demonstrating you heard:

- Hearing what they are *not* saying.
- Uncovering the actual problem behind a stated solution. Stakeholders naturally think in solutions ("move this button"); the designer's job is to surface the underlying problem (proximity to the date picker, not button placement).
- Not interrupting.
- Mastering the pause — letting silence do the work of inviting more thinking, both yours and theirs.

When you let stakeholders talk as much as they want, you communicate that you appreciate what they are saying. This builds trust and makes later agreement more likely.

### Explicit Listening

Skills that visibly demonstrate you heard:

- Taking notes (visibly).
- Asking clarifying questions, especially questions that translate stakeholder solutions back into problems.
- Repeating or rephrasing what was said to confirm understanding before responding.

Without explicit listening, stakeholders cannot tell whether they have been heard, and they will keep restating the same point in slightly different forms. With explicit listening, the meeting moves on.

## Form A Response

Only after listening is it appropriate to respond. The book's structure for response work is to organize your thoughts based on what you actually heard, not on the response you rehearsed before the meeting. The response should:

- Address the *underlying problem*, not the surface-level solution the stakeholder proposed.
- Be grounded in the data, research, or alternatives you brought.
- Acknowledge the stakeholder values you identified in preparation.
- Be willing to commit to a next step.

A good response makes the design decision look like a shared choice rather than an imposition.

## The Meeting After The Meeting

People do not always say what they think in front of the group. Reasons in the book: deference to the boss, fear of unpopular or risky positions, social-status calculations. Many decisions actually get made in the corridor afterward.

Operating rule: **stay around for a few minutes after the meeting**. Chat, thank people for participating, see what comes up. If the meeting is remote, stay on the call or message a key participant for a quick debrief. The book's claim is that as much gets done after the meeting as during it.

A quick follow-up note within the day:

1. Thanks the attendees for their time and participation.
2. Summarizes what was decided.
3. Closes with actions, next steps, and expectations — not with a recap, but with what happens next.

Communicating *what happens next* makes the meeting feel like a good use of time and offloads the burden onto the team rather than keeping it on you.

## Filter Comments That Do Not Align

Not all stakeholder comments deserve to drive the project. The book's pharmaceutical-AR example — an executive who suggested an augmented-reality store map that the designer knew was off-strategy — is a case study in *deciding what to filter*. If a comment:

- Does not align with project objectives, *and*
- Is not from an influential decision-maker, *and*
- Did not gain agreement from anyone else in the room, *and*
- Is unlikely to be raised again,

it is safe to leave out of follow-up documentation. The point is not to disrespect the speaker; it is to avoid letting noise cloud the decision record.

## Anti-Patterns

- Pinning mockups to a wall and asking "what do you think?" — this invites knee-jerk reactions and demeans the work.
- Two equally weighted "primary" options shown without a recommendation. Stakeholders are not designers; do not require them to pick.
- Lorem ipsum in a stakeholder review (see the pharmaceutical-app anecdote).
- Skipping the post-meeting follow-up; the after-meeting is half the work.
- Treating disagreement as resistance rather than as data about stakeholder values.

## How The Engine Uses This Playbook

When the user is preparing to communicate a UX content or design decision:

1. Re-state stakeholder values and stories for each person who will be in the room.
2. Pre-write anticipated reactions and prepared responses.
3. Recommend bringing alternatives, data, and research.
4. Sketch the meeting agenda using primacy/recency: first frame, middle content, final reinforcement.
5. Coach implicit and explicit listening behaviors.
6. Provide a follow-up template that thanks, summarizes, and names next steps.
7. Flag filter-eligible comments before they get into the project record.

## Source Grounding

- The book's framing of all design communication around a single meeting with stakeholders/clients, with chapters mapped to before, during, and after.
- The "stakeholders are people too" chapter: stakeholder values and stakeholder stories as the preparation lens.
- "Anticipate Reactions": common sense + observed reactions = predictable behavior, paired with bringing alternatives, data, and research.
- Implicit vs. explicit listening: implicit = no outward signals required, including hearing what is not said and the art of the pause; explicit = note-taking, questioning, rephrasing.
- "Design the Meeting": setting context at the start, naming the meeting honestly, using primacy and recency, removing distractions like outdated mockups and lorem ipsum.
- "The Meeting After the Meeting": staying behind, debriefing, follow-up notes that thank, summarize, and name next steps; filtering off-strategy comments such as the pharmaceutical-AR example.
