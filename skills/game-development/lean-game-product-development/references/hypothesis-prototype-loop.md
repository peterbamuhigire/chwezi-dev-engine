# Hypothesis and Prototype Loop

Parent: [Lean Game Product Development](../SKILL.md)

This self-contained reference distils the supplied second edition of *Lean Game Development* into an evidence-gated workflow. Named case studies and dated service/tool claims from the book are not treated as current authority.

## Lean operating principles for games

Apply these as decision tests:

- Eliminate work that neither ships value nor reduces uncertainty.
- Build quality into the experiment so defects do not invalidate the signal.
- Generate and preserve team knowledge through reviews, tests and concise records.
- Delay irreversible commitments while evidence is cheap.
- Deliver the next learning unit quickly.
- Respect participants, contributors and the production team.
- Optimise the complete player-and-production system, not one department's local output.

Limit work in progress. An unfinished prototype, unreviewed art set or unanalysed playtest is inventory that hides risk.

## Inception contract

A bounded inception answers:

| Question | Output |
|---|---|
| Who is the player and what should they feel/do? | player promise and experience pillars |
| What must remain true? | non-negotiables and non-goals |
| What could kill the project? | ranked assumption/risk backlog |
| What is the smallest complete proof? | prototype sequence and first gate |
| Who decides? | decision owner and review cadence |
| What evidence is ethical and feasible? | method, consent, device and cultural constraints |

Keep alternative concepts alive until the cost of comparison exceeds the decision value. Record why the chosen concept won.

## Hypothesis anatomy

A usable game hypothesis contains:

1. Target player/context.
2. Build or intervention.
3. Expected observable behaviour or outcome.
4. Threshold and measurement method.
5. Time box and sample/context boundary.
6. Competing explanation or failure mode.
7. Decision if pass, mixed or fail.

Example structure: `For [target context], when [prototype condition], we expect [observable signal] to meet [threshold] under [method]. A pass permits [bounded decision]; a fail causes [narrow/pivot/stop].`

Do not use a subjective adjective such as fun, intuitive or cinematic as the signal. Operationalise it through observed continuation, comprehension, error, mastery, recall, preference with reasons, or another prespecified measure.

## Prototype ladder

| Prototype | Use | Avoid claiming |
|---|---|---|
| Paper/rules model | choice structure, economy, information flow | control feel or rendering feasibility |
| Greybox | movement, camera, timing, spatial comprehension | final visual appeal |
| Technical spike | performance/API/pipeline feasibility | player value |
| Art/audio specimen | mood, readability, pipeline and device fit | complete loop quality |
| Isolated mechanic | one verb or system interaction | production scalability |
| Playable prototype | integrated fun/comprehension question | representative production cost unless disciplines are representative |
| Vertical slice | player promise plus production pipeline evidence | full-game demand or schedule certainty |

An MVG/minimum lovable game is a coherent player-facing value unit assembled from validated prototypes. It is not every desired feature at low quality.

## Learning consolidation

After each decision:

- promote validated behaviour into controlled requirements and test cases;
- record invalidated assumptions and removed scope;
- preserve reusable tools/assets only if they meet production standards;
- update cost/throughput estimates from actual work;
- add defects caused by missing standards to the relevant skill or checklist;
- keep raw observations separate from interpretation.

Do not expand production while the team cannot explain what the experiment changed.
