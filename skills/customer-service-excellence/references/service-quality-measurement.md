# Service Quality Measurement

The case studies of marketing and service performance show a consistent pattern: high performers live a measurement culture. Measurement sits at the centre of the organisation, not at the edges. Marketing and service measurement is a board-level agenda item; the team accelerates its learning through systematic test-and-learn; insights and best practices are shared across the organisation rather than hoarded in one team. Toyota's marketing director put it directly: the team is empowered to do its job, but they know they are being watched - because the board does not look at individual campaigns, but does receive the key performance indicators every cycle.

The discipline this reference describes is how to design a service measurement system that produces decisions, not slides. The default failure mode in service measurement is the opposite: a wall of metrics that everyone tracks and no one acts on, with vanity numbers crowding out the few measurements that would change behaviour.

## Principle 1 - Tie every metric back to a small number of KPIs

Toyota's example: many measurements feed direct campaigns, but they all tie back to common key performance indicators that demonstrate prospect engagement with the brand - test drives taken, brochure requests made. The discipline is the tying-back, not the count of metrics.

For a service operation, that means:
- Define no more than five organisation-level service KPIs that the leadership team genuinely uses for decisions.
- Every operational metric tracked anywhere in the operation must roll up, cleanly, to one of those five.
- Metrics that do not roll up are either (a) wrong, (b) duplicative, or (c) operating-team specific and should not be reported above their owner.

Anti-pattern: presenting forty metrics on a dashboard "so executives can drill in". Forty metrics is no metrics; the executives stop reading.

## Principle 2 - Measure customer relationship segments, not just transactions

Tesco's loyalty programme, run with Dunnhumby, did not measure customers as one undifferentiated population. It segmented by commitment profile - cautious / developing / committed / loyal - and the company's stated aim was to move customers through these profiles as they progressed along the customer journey. Communications, offers, and measurement were tailored to where the customer was on that journey.

The implication for service measurement: a satisfaction score is more useful when it is decomposed by relationship stage. The first-shop customer's tolerance for failure is different from the fourth-shop customer's; the at-risk customer's signals look different from the loyal customer's. A single average obscures all of this.

Tesco's specific finding is instructive: getting customers through registration and the first shop was not enough; they were still in danger of lapsing until they reached the magic "fourth shop", at which point they became habitual customers with potential to move up to the higher commitment levels. The lesson: define the threshold transactions that signal stickiness, and measure progress to those thresholds, not just satisfaction at any moment.

## Principle 3 - The four-metric core

Limit the headline service measurement set to four classes, each with a defined role:

### A. Resolution effectiveness (the operational metric)

What gets measured: did the customer's stated need get resolved on this contact, and within an acceptable time?

This is the closest analogue to first-contact resolution. It is necessary but insufficient: a high resolution-effectiveness rate accompanied by low long-term retention indicates that contacts are being closed without the customer's underlying issue being addressed.

Failure mode if used alone: agents close tickets prematurely to hit the target.

### B. Effort score (the experience metric)

What gets measured: how much work did the customer have to do to get the issue resolved? Specifically, how many channels were used, how many times the customer had to repeat themselves, how many handoffs occurred.

The anti-target is the voicemail-from-hell journey: a customer routed around, repeating themselves, before reaching anyone with authority. High effort scores correlate strongly with churn even when resolution scores look fine.

Failure mode if used alone: agents over-resolve simple issues to keep effort scores low.

### C. Loyalty / advocacy (the long-arc metric)

What gets measured: would the customer recommend / continue / expand?

This is where Net Promoter or its equivalent sits. It is most useful when sliced by relationship stage (per Tesco) and segment, and when changes are tracked over time rather than reported as a single number. Famous Grouse's tracking studies, for example, did not just report a satisfaction number; they reported the share of respondents who agreed the brand was "a brand worth paying more for", which is closer to behaviour than feeling.

Failure mode if used alone: vanity metric prone to gaming via timing of survey send. Eric Ries's warning about vanity metrics applies directly: a metric whose only function is to make people feel good is worse than no metric, because it crowds out actionable measurement.

### D. Recovery success (the asymmetric metric)

What gets measured: of customers who experienced a service failure, what proportion continued to do business at the same or greater rate after recovery?

Recovery success is the asymmetric metric because the population is small and the consequences are large. A service operation with a low overall failure rate but poor recovery success will see disproportionate churn from the few customers whose first failure went unaddressed. Conversely, a high recovery rate creates an unexpected loyalty effect: customers whose problems are well-recovered often become more loyal than those who never had a problem.

Failure mode if used alone: organisations stop investing in failure prevention because recovery numbers look good.

## Principle 4 - Lagging plus leading

Hubbard's warning, cited in the strategy literature: information is valuable when it reduces uncertainty about a decision, and the value of measuring a variable is often inversely proportional to the attention it gets. The metrics that get the most attention are usually the easiest to measure, not the most decision-relevant.

For each of the four headline metrics above, identify a leading indicator that moves before the lagging metric does:

- Resolution effectiveness lag → first-attempt diagnosis quality (lead).
- Effort score lag → handoff count per ticket (lead).
- Loyalty lag → product usage frequency, second-purchase probability (lead).
- Recovery success lag → follow-up completion rate on promised callbacks (lead).

The leading indicator is what the operation can act on inside a week. The lagging metric tells you whether the leading indicator was the right thing to act on.

## Principle 5 - Two-level metrics (the Scott trap)

The strategy literature warns about the conflict between two levels of metrics: the ones that provide control to people outside the process, and the ones that are meaningful to the people inside. When outside metrics dominate, people inside the process focus on hitting the outside number even when it damages the underlying objective.

For service operations, this means publishing two metric sets:
- **Outside metrics**: the four headline metrics, used by leadership for control.
- **Inside metrics**: defined by frontline teams, meaningful to their day-to-day work, used for improvement.

The two sets must be reconcilable but not identical. If frontline measurement is purely the outside metrics imposed downward, the team will optimise the metrics and erode the underlying experience.

## Principle 6 - Cadence (review rhythm)

A measurement system without a review rhythm produces dashboards, not decisions. The minimum cadence:
- Weekly operational review: leading indicators, by team.
- Monthly experience review: the four headline metrics by segment.
- Quarterly strategy review: trend across two quarters, with explicit "what would we change" question on each.

Each review must end with a written next action, an owner, and a review date - or it does not count as a review.

## What does not belong in the headline set

- Volume metrics (calls handled, tickets closed) - these are workload, not quality.
- Speed metrics in isolation - "average handle time" without resolution data rewards rushing.
- Satisfaction scores from non-representative samples (only happy customers respond) - vanity metric.
- Awards, mentions, sentiment counts - peripheral cues, not decision inputs.

## Decision rules

- No more than five organisational service KPIs.
- Every operational metric must roll up to one.
- Always pair a lagging metric with a leading indicator.
- Always present the four headline metrics by relationship segment, not in aggregate alone.
- Every review meeting ends with a named next action and an owner.
- Anti-pattern: a metric whose movement nobody acts on - retire it.
