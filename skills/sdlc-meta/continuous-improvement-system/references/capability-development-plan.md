# Capability Development Plan

A continuous-improvement system depends on the capability of the people running it. The strategy literature is direct: the most effective strategists make progress not by performing high-prestige decision rituals but by performing the basics - writing things down, exploring widely, incorporating feedback from people who disagree. That is a description of capability, not seniority. The case studies of marketing and service excellence concur: high performers excel in hiring the right individuals and investing in their development through training that reinforces an evidence-driven culture, with an analytical mindset and commercial perspective treated as essential rather than as nice-to-haves.

This reference describes how to plan and run a capability programme that actually moves capability, rather than one that consumes training budget while producing little.

## Three capability dimensions

A capability programme that addresses only one dimension is partial and will fail. The three:

### 1. Skills (what individuals can do)

The technical and behavioural abilities of named individuals. For a continuous-improvement function, the load-bearing skills include: facilitating workshops with mixed business and technical groups, defining good measurements (scale, meter, benchmark, constraint, target), separating goals from features, distinguishing earn from learn, designing inspection mechanisms that fail loudly, writing strategy clearly enough to be argued.

The discipline: name the skills the function depends on, name who has each skill at what level, and identify the gaps.

### 2. Tools and data (what the system supports)

The instrumentation that makes capability operational. For continuous improvement: dashboards with the load-bearing metrics, a strategy archive, a written record of past retrospectives, the means to run inspection mechanisms asynchronously, the templates for impact maps and milestone plans.

A team with capable people but no tools cannot scale; a team with tools but no skills produces dashboards that nobody uses.

### 3. Practices (what the team does together)

The routines and norms that turn skills and tools into outcomes. Cadence of reviews, the standard format for a retrospective, the convention for what gets written down and what does not, the norm about who is in the room for which kind of decision.

The strategy literature's "model, document, share" approach captures the practice dimension: model the approach you want others to adopt, document it with the thinking behind it, share the document around. Practices propagate through demonstration, not through edict.

## What good capability investment looks like

The marketing-excellence finding: creating a measurement culture is not easy, does not happen quickly, and does not happen on its own. It requires deliberate development of skills, data, and technology with an eye toward building a long-term competitive capability.

Concrete forms that work:

- **Hire for analytical mindset and commercial perspective.** The case studies are explicit about this; it is the differentiating attribute. Without analytical capability, the team cannot interpret evidence; without commercial perspective, they cannot tie evidence to decisions that matter.
- **Embed evidence-driven decision-making in onboarding.** New people learn how the team actually thinks about evidence, not just the local jargon.
- **Make measurement boardroom-visible.** When measurement is a board agenda item (Diageo, Toyota), the team treats it as load-bearing. When it is buried in a quarterly report, the team treats it as paperwork.
- **Empower the team to act, then watch.** Toyota's marketing director's framing: "the team is empowered to do its job, but they know they are being watched." Empowerment without inspection produces drift; inspection without empowerment produces compliance theatre.

## What does not work

The strategy literature is direct on the failure modes of capability investment:

- **Mandatory annual training.** Two compounding problems: because attendance is required, trainers stop investing in quality; trainees stop paying attention because they expect low-quality content. The result is people trained annually on a policy who report never having heard of it. Use sparingly; never as the primary capability mechanism.
- **Edict-driven capability**. Telling people to "be more strategic" or "use evidence" without modelling what that looks like, documenting the practice, or building the supporting tools. Capability does not propagate by instruction.
- **Single-axis programmes.** Skills programmes without tools, tool rollouts without skills, practice mandates without either. Each fails because the other axes are absent.

## The four-step capability development cycle

### Step 1 - Diagnose the capability state

Run a structured diagnostic across the three dimensions. For each load-bearing skill, name the people who can do it and at what level. For each tool, score whether it is in use, partially in use, or theoretical. For each practice, score whether it is consistent, inconsistent, or absent.

The output is a small number of named gaps - not "we need more analytics", but "no one in the team can run a measurement-design workshop with a non-technical sponsor", or "we have a strategy archive but nobody refers to it during planning".

### Step 2 - Pick the highest-leverage gap

The strategy-refinement principle applies here: do not try to fix everything at once. Pick the gap whose closure would unlock the most other gaps.

The Stripe Sorbet example from the strategy literature is instructive: a team of 10 engineers pushed a major change incrementally without distracting the larger organisation. The discipline was choosing the highest-leverage initiative, scoping it to a small team, and executing without trying to boil the ocean. The same logic applies to capability investment: a small, focused, high-leverage capability initiative outperforms a broad transformation programme.

### Step 3 - Run the closing initiative across all three dimensions

Whatever the gap is, address all three dimensions of it:

- **Skills**: who learns what, by when, demonstrated how. Demonstrated, not certified. The test is whether the person can do the work, not whether they have a certificate.
- **Tools**: what supporting instrumentation is built or bought.
- **Practices**: what routine carries the new capability into daily work, so it does not evaporate when the initial investment ends.

A common shape: pair a senior practitioner with two or three people who need to learn the skill, build the supporting tool together as part of the learning, and inscribe the new practice in the cadence (weekly, monthly, or quarterly review).

### Step 4 - Inspect the result

Capability investment, like any other strategy, needs an inspection mechanism. Three months after the initiative shipped, can the people demonstrably do the thing? Are the tools in active use? Has the practice survived contact with normal operating pressure?

Run the inspection asynchronously where possible (the dashboard-and-channel pattern). Synchronous reviews of capability progress are expensive and tend to produce optimistic-sounding readouts.

## Cross-cutting capability themes

### Writing as the primary capability mechanism

The strategy literature's argument that the single biggest action you can take to further strategy is to write strategy down so that the organisation can debate it, agree upon it, and explicitly evolve - applies broadly. Writing is the primary capability mechanism. Teams that do not write down their thinking re-derive it every quarter. Teams that write it down can argue it, refine it, and pass it on.

Practices that strengthen writing capability:
- Every meaningful decision has a one-page written record before it ships.
- Every retrospective produces a written next-action list with owners.
- Every strategy review produces a timestamped record of what changed since last review.
- New hires read the written archive in onboarding, then write a critique of one document as part of joining.

### Disagreement as the test of clarity

A written strategy that "everyone agrees with" is often ambiguous. The strategy literature: making it possible to disagree more precisely is one of the key benefits of having a written strategy. Capability is partly the ability to surface and process disagreement productively.

Practices that build this capability:
- Welcome disagreement during reviews; name agreement-without-engagement as a failure mode.
- Use red-team / blue-team formats for important decisions.
- Document the cases where the team chose against a senior leader's instinct, with the reasoning, so that future teams can see the precedent.

### Information herd immunity

Capability does not require everyone to know everything. The strategy literature's term: information dispersal in an organisation is like herd immunity - you do not need everyone to know something, you just need enough people to know it that confusion does not propagate too far.

Implication for capability planning: target the staff-plus-engineer or equivalent layer rather than universal training, and accept that some teams will learn from the people who learned first.

## Decision rules

- A capability gap that addresses only one dimension is a partial fix and will not stick.
- Pick the highest-leverage gap and execute fully, rather than addressing many gaps shallowly.
- Hire for analytical mindset and commercial perspective; train the rest.
- Mandatory annual training is rarely the right answer; in-context, demonstrated practice usually is.
- Writing is the primary capability mechanism. If a capability cannot be written down, it cannot be transferred.
- Schedule a three-month inspection of any capability initiative; capability that is not inspected drifts.
